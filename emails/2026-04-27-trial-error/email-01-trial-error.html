4–5 EMC test attempts is now considered normal.
It shouldn't be.

Dario Fresu

Principal EMC Architect · Fresu Electronics

Somewhere around the third failed EMC test, teams stop calling it failure.

They call it "trial and error" instead. Four attempts, sometimes five. Each one a lab slot, a redesign, and a delay that moves the launch date further right.

That reframe is expensive — because it turns a planning failure into an accepted part of the certification process.

What four attempts actually costs

Per failed attempt

Lab fees. Engineering time. Travel. A PCB revision nobody planned for. At €40–80K per cycle — and 6–10 weeks per re-spin — three extra attempts is a six-figure hole in a schedule that was already tight.

That number doesn't include the market window you lose when the competitor who planned differently ships three months ahead of you.

Why it keeps happening

Not because EMC is unpredictable. Because most products arrive at the test chamber without ever having had a real EMC review. The lab becomes the first one.

At that point you're debugging under the worst possible conditions: mechanical frozen, launch date public, every fix requiring you to undo decisions made six months ago.

The window that still matters

If you're 6–12 months from a certification deadline, the decisions that matter are still revisable. The schedule can still absorb a design change.
