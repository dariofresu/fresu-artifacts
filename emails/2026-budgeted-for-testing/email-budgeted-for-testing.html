Hardware Engineering · EMC

Most hardware products that fail EMC certification weren't designed wrong.
They were planned wrong.

Dario Fresu

Principal EMC Architect · Fresu Electronics

I've been through the project timelines of every company I've audited in the last two years.

EMC isn't in any of them.

There's usually a line that says "testing" near the end. Maybe a week blocked before the submission date. Sometimes not even that.

That's not the same thing.

What actually happens when EMC is not in the plan

By the time anyone on the team is thinking seriously about compliance, mechanical is frozen. Production is lined up. The launch date is in the press release — and sometimes already in a customer contract.

At that point, every available fix costs more than it should and takes longer than you have.

The average cost

A failed EMC test costs €40–80K in redesign and retest. Add 3–6 months of delay. A product that was already sold to a customer that's now waiting.

The worst part: none of that had to happen.

The same product. The same team. Different outcome.

Every team I've worked with that cleared certification on the first attempt planned EMC in from week one. Not perfectly — nobody does it perfectly. But the stack-up was chosen with return path impedance in mind. The filter topology was specified before the schematic was frozen. The connector selection included common-mode characteristics.

None of these decisions are expensive early. They're expensive only when they're made late — or not made at all and discovered in the chamber.

It's not a technical problem at that point. It's a planning problem with a technical invoice.
