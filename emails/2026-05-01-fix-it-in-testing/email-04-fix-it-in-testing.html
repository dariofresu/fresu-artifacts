"We'll fix it in testing."
What that sentence costs when the lab disagrees.

Dario Fresu

Principal EMC Architect · Fresu Electronics

TL;DR — This week's insight

How first-pass teams use "we'll fix it in testing" — and how most teams use it differently.

1.  EMC constraints are in the project plan before layout starts — trade-offs get made with data, not deferred to the lab

2.  Pre-compliance runs at prototype stage — problems found at €2–5K instead of €40–80K

3.  When "we'll fix it in testing" gets said, it's a deliberate call based on actual data — not a way to get past the next deadline

Every EMC re-spin I've seen started with the same four words.

It usually comes from a project plan with no room for EMC trade-offs. When a layout decision needs to be made and the EMC constraints aren't written down anywhere, the easy move is to log it and keep going. So teams do. Three months later, the lab finds it at €40–80K a cycle.

The fix, when it comes, is usually straightforward. What costs is doing it at the wrong stage — after the board is locked, after the schedule is tight, after you've already told your customer when the product ships.

The cost of when

Pre-compliance at prototype stage costs €2–5K. A failed EMC test cycle costs €40–80K plus 3–6 months of schedule. The problem is rarely the fix. It's when you find it.

What first-pass teams say instead

They use the phrase too. But when they say it, they've already looked at the risk. The fix is bounded. They know what the lab might find and have decided it's manageable. That's not the same as saying it to get past a deadline.

The reason they can make that call is that EMC constraints were in the project plan before the layout started. Present when the component list was locked. When the first schematic review happened. When the timeline still had room to move.

Most teams

"We'll fix it in testing" closes the trade-off discussion

EMC added to the schedule 4–6 weeks before submission

Re-spin budget approved after the lab confirms the failure

Customer told about the delay after it's already happened

First-pass teams

"We'll fix it in testing" is a decision — made with EMC data, not instead of it

EMC constraints in the plan before component selection

Re-spin budget is a project plan line item, not a surprise

Risk flagged to leadership before the lab confirms it

First-pass teams still make trade-offs. Every project has them. What's different is that when they walk into the lab, none of it is news to them. They know what they carried in and why.
