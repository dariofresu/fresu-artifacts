Single Conductor Fields
The starting point — how E and B fields surround one isolated conductor
Why Start Here

Before understanding EMI in PCBs, you need to understand what a single conductor does to the surrounding space. Without a second conductor to terminate the fields, both the magnetic and electric fields expand freely to infinity. This is an antenna. This is the origin of EMI.

Reference — Electric and Magnetic Fields in a Signal Conductor (Steinmetz, 1911). Blue solid circles = Magnetic flux Φ (closed loops). Dashed radial lines = Electric flux Ψ (radiates outward).
Highlight:
Interactive — Field Diagram
Magnetic field Φ = solid concentric rings — closed loops, always. Electric field Ψ = dashed straight radial lines — radiates freely without termination. Toggle the buttons above to isolate each field.
Magnetic Field Φ

Always Closed Loops

Magnetic field lines always form closed loops encircling the conductor. They constitute a magnetic circuit. Their density decreases with distance. They can never start or end at a point.

Electric Field Ψ

Radiates Without Termination

Around a single conductor, electric field lines radiate outward as straight lines with no termination point — they expand to infinity. This is a radiating antenna. This is what a PCB trace without an adjacent RRP looks like electromagnetically.

Two-Conductor System
Adding the return conductor fundamentally transforms the field geometry
The Critical Transition

With two conductors at opposite polarity, electric field lines originate at one conductor and terminate at the other. They no longer radiate to infinity. Magnetic field lines concentrate in the space between the conductors. This is the beginning of field containment.

Reference — Two-conductor system (Steinmetz, 1911, p.11). The two conductors are side by side. Magnetic field Φ (solid circles) concentrates between them. Electric field Ψ (dashed lines) now originates at one conductor and terminates at the other — fields no longer radiate to infinity.

The return conductor at opposite polarity terminates the electric field lines and concentrates the magnetic field between them. This is the foundation of EMI control in PCBs.

PCB Cross-Section
The two-conductor system applied to a PCB trace above a Return Reference Plane
The Riverbank Analogy

Think of the conductors as riverbanks. They don't carry the water — they contain and direct it. In PCB design, the signal trace and Return Reference Plane are the riverbanks. The electromagnetic fields are the river flowing in the dielectric between them. Your job is to design the riverbanks correctly.

Left: PCB trace without RRP — fields expand in all directions (EMI radiator). Right: Signal trace above Return Reference Plane — fields concentrate in the dielectric between them. The RRP changes everything.
Left: 2-layer PCB cross-section — Conductor 1 (signal trace), dielectric, Conductor 2 (RRP). Signal propagates as EM fields through the dielectric. Right: The river analogy — the riverbanks (conductors) channel the river (EM fields) between them.

The rotation: The PCB trace + RRP is exactly the two-conductor system from Module 2 — rotated 90° so the conductors are flat and horizontal. The signal trace is the top conductor. The RRP is the bottom conductor. The dielectric in between is where the energy propagates — not in the copper.

The Dielectric

More Important Than the Copper

The copper guides the fields. The energy propagates in the FR4 dielectric. The choice of stackup — dielectric material, layer spacing, RRP continuity — determines EMI performance. The copper is just the riverbank.

Design Implication

Route Half a Transmission Line

Every trace you route is one conductor of a two-conductor system. The return path is the other conductor. Neglect it and the fields will find their own return path — expanding to earth ground, creating large radiating loops.

The Two Models
The model you were taught — and the one that actually explains EMI
How they teach you: electricity is the flow of electrons inside conductors. Electrons move from + to load through the signal conductor, then return via the ground conductor. This model explains DC circuits — but cannot explain EMI, radiation, or propagation speed.
How it actually works: electricity is the flow of electromagnetic (EM) fields in the space between conductors. The Poynting vector shows energy flowing between — not inside — the conductors. The copper boundaries guide and contain the fields.
✗ Wrong — Electron Flow

Electricity = electrons flowing through copper. Source → signal conductor → load → return conductor. Explains Ohm's law and DC only.

Why it fails: Cannot explain why signals travel at ~½ the speed of light. Cannot explain EMI, radiation, or why an adjacent ground plane reduces emissions.

✓ Correct — EM Fields

Electricity = EM fields propagating in the space between conductors. Copper guides the fields. Poynting vector defines energy direction.

Why it matters: Explains radiation, field containment, why the RRP matters, why plane splits cause EMI, and how to design for low emissions.

RRP & Field Containment
What the Return Reference Plane does — and what happens when it's absent
2-layer PCB with a solid, continuous Return Reference Plane adjacent to the signal layer. EMC radiated emission test result: PASSED. Fields are contained in the dielectric between signal trace and RRP. Return current flows directly under the trace.
2-layer PCB where both layers are used for signals — no Return Reference Plane. EMC radiated emission test result: FAILED. Fields from both signal layers expand freely, contaminate each other, and exceed EMC limits.
The First Rule of PCB EMC Design

For every signal layer, there must be a Return Reference Plane adjacent to it. That plane must be solid, continuous, and free of holes, cuts, or gaps. Without this, the PCB becomes a radiating antenna. This single rule is responsible for more EMC pass/fail outcomes than any other design decision.

With Solid RRP

Fields Contained — EMC Passed

Electric fields propagate in the dielectric between trace and RRP. Return current flows directly under the trace. Minimum loop area, minimum radiation. EMC test: PASSED.

Without RRP

PCB Becomes a Radiator — EMC Failed

Fields from signal layers expand outward and contaminate each other. Common mode currents form over large loops. EMC limits exceeded. EMC test: FAILED.

No Adjacent RRP

Earth Ground as Return Path

Fields keep expanding until they find earth ground or nearby conductors. This creates enormous current loops — the most efficient radiating antenna possible. Earth ground is never an acceptable RRP substitute at frequency.

RRP Must Be Solid

No Cuts, Holes, or Splits

Any discontinuity in the RRP forces return current to detour, creating a radiating loop. The RRP must be as integral and as close as possible to the signal trace — solid, continuous, no gaps.
